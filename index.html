📖 Biography

🖋️📚

Franz Kafka, c. 1906

Franz Kafka (3 July 1883 – 3 June 1924) was a German-speaking Bohemian novelist and short-story writer, widely regarded as one of the major figures of 20th-century literature.

Born into a middle-class German-speaking Jewish family in Prague, Kafka worked as an insurance officer while writing in his spare time. He published only a few short stories during his lifetime — most of his major works were published posthumously by his friend Max Brod, against Kafka's wishes to have them destroyed.

Kafka died of tuberculosis at age 40. His work — blending realism with the fantastical, absurd, and nightmarish — gave rise to the term "Kafkaesque".

📚 Major Works

The Metamorphosis

1915 (Die Verwandlung)

Gregor Samsa wakes up transformed into a giant insect. A surreal exploration of alienation, family duty, and identity.

The Trial

1925 (posthumous)

Josef K. is arrested and prosecuted by a mysterious, inaccessible court. The ultimate nightmare of bureaucracy.

The Castle

1926 (posthumous)

A land surveyor struggles against an elusive, authoritarian castle bureaucracy. Unfinished masterpiece.

In the Penal Colony

1919

A chilling short story about a torture machine that inscribes judgment directly onto the condemned.

Letter to His Father

1919

A 100-page letter analyzing his difficult relationship with his domineering father.

A Hunger Artist

1922

A professional faster whose art becomes obsolete — a metaphor for the misunderstood artist.

"Kafkaesque" describes situations marked by surreal, oppressive, or nightmarish qualities — especially bureaucratic labyrinths where logic fails and individuals are crushed by incomprehensible systems.

🌟 Literary Legacy

Kafka's influence extends far beyond literature:

  • Philosophy: Existentialism, absurdism (Camus, Sartre)
  • Literature: García Márquez, Borges, Murakami, Coetzee
  • Film: Terry Gilliam's "Brazil," Orson Welles' "The Trial"
  • Language: "Kafkaesque" entered English dictionaries
  • Adaptations: Countless plays, operas, graphic novels, films
"Kafka is important to us because his predicament is the predicament of modern man."
— W.H. Auden

🎤 Presentation Tips

For your Kafka presentation:

  • Start with a striking quote or the opening of The Metamorphosis
  • Use visuals of Prague, old photographs, or surreal art
  • Explain "Kafkaesque" with everyday examples (DMV, bureaucracy, confusing forms)
  • Contrast Kafka's quiet life with his explosive literary imagination
  • Discuss why he wanted his works destroyed — and why we're grateful Max Brod disobeyed
